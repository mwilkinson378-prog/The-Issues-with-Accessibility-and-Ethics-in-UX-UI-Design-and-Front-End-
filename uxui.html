Discipline 01: The Architect

UX/UI Design
Accessibility & Ethics

UX/UI design is not a neutral aesthetic choice. It actively connects with human behavior, social interactions, and individual navigation for social issues. Digital products have the power to either grow user autonomy through inclusivity and a transparent framework or destroy it through deceptive practices and technical exclusion.

The Current Landscape of Deception

UX/UI design currently has pressure where the engagement metrics and stakeholders' benefit often outweigh the fundamental necessity of the safety of users. As documented in "Manipulative, Dark, and Unethical Design Practices," the current digital environment is increasingly growing with interfaces and code to undermine user autonomy and negatively impact user behavior and well being.

This shift can lead to corruption of digital ethics, as ethical and legal issues often struggle to keep up with modern development and new technology. When designer priortize hacking or dark tactics, for example, “attention theft” and “coercive friction”, they choose to contribute to a pervasive culture of distrust.

The rise of “dark patterns” as an industry practice shows a disregard for the intent of users in favor of psychological vulnerability for the stakeholder. The design choices are not acceptable. As highlighted by Virginia Tech, these choices aren't accidental; they are "crafted to manipulate." A designer realizes that the responsibility for these ethical breaches falls on the designers and developers, even though many developers report that external pressure often influences their decision to implement these harmful patterns.

The Inclusivity Mandate

As cited in "Accessibility for Designers and UX Researchers," accessibility and inclusivity are indivisible. The current design can use “trickery” through deceptive patterns, while an ethical product prioritizes responsiveness and readability. Choosing personal aesthetic over up to code standards created barriers that lock out portions of the population from viewing websites. Per ADA, people with limited vision cannot read text if there is not enough contrast .

ADA Standard

“It can be hard for some people with vision disabilities to see text on a web page if there is not enough contrast between the color of the text and the background color."

Deceptive Patterns: The "Dark" UI

Pattern 01

Visual Trickery

Visual trickery consists of UI tricks for crafting that influence or manipulate users into doing things they might not consent to. The design choices that are made are not accidental. They are created to exploit a users psychological vulnerabilities in the benefit of the stakeholder at the expense of the user. (Liang 2025)

Pattern 02

The Roach Motel

The Roach Motel is a dark pattern in which the sources define UI choices "crafted to manipulate or trick users into actions that they are not intended to do". This pattern focuses on understanding user autonomy by making it easy to get stuck in situations. Situations like subscriptions and data sharing agreements while making it it hard to leave.

Pattern 03

Misdirection

Misdirection is a tactic in deceptive design. UI elements are specifically "crafted to manipulate or trick users into situations they don't knowingly enter. Designers use visuals to guide a user through a specific action to serve a business rather than the needs of the user. This design exploits psychological vulnerabilities for stakeholders.

The Stakeholder Conflict

An issue of "Understanding User and Developer Perceptions" is the tension between ethical design and business KPIs. Designers can find themselves at a crossroads between two bad decisions:

Stakeholder Pressure

Prioritizes "Growth Hacking," high conversion rates, and retention at any cost to the user experience.

Ethical Responsibility

Prioritizes user autonomy, informed consent, and long-term trust as defined by the ADA and WCAG.

When these goals clash, "Dark Patterns" are often the result of prioritizing short-term metrics over human-centric standards.

The Architecture of Deception: A Dual Audit

Executive Summary

AI systems often suffer from systemic "algorithmic opacity.” This creates a “black box” effect where the location and logic of decisions are obscure from real designer oversight. This lack of transparency causes many issues. It creates a mirrored failure of “responsiveness” where automated interfaces neglect accessibility like WCAG. This would be due to the failure of prioritizing an adaptable and traceable design; AI developers risk creating “accessibility barriers” that lock out populations from online resources.
